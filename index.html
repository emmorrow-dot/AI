Staff
9 people
▸
Top pain points
Personnel capacity: Roles are not being backfilled after departures, leaving everyone doing more with less. There is no redundancy in IT or AV support, and burnout is a consistent theme across this group.
HR feels dehumanized: The shift to ticketing systems for HR removes the human connection people value. Staff want to reach a person they know, not submit a ticket into a queue.
Open office and lack of private space: Glass walls and an open layout make staff feel constantly accessible and interrupted, making focused work difficult. Work-from-home days are often the only opportunity for deep work.
System fragmentation: Teams rely on manual data transfer between disconnected platforms. There is no bandwidth or organizational support to address the integrations needed.
Top positives
Community and colleagues · Physical environment · Hybrid flexibility · Internal communications that surface accomplishments and events help reduce isolation
Unique to this group
Envoy underdeployment · Donor data inaccessibility · Burnout as a retention risk · Loss of weekly team meetings · Event Intake Form reliability · Student employee turnover creating inconsistent event support · Social connection gaps (Fikas valued; more informal gatherings needed)
Research faculty, scientists & postdocs
7 people
▸
Top pain points
No private offices: Constant movement between phone booths and meeting rooms throughout the day is impractical and has gotten worse over time. Some researchers spend entire campus days in a pod because the open area does not support writing or focused meetings.
Meeting space scarcity and enforcement: Rooms that appear available in the booking system are often physically occupied with no enforcement mechanism. A/V reliability in classrooms is a related and unresolved issue.
Siloed groups and unclear structure: Research groups rarely mix across divisions. Organizational structure is unclear even for long-tenured staff. Postdocs in particular receive less information than faculty about how to progress and what resources are available to them.
Top positives
NU IT infrastructure (HPC, journal access, Claude enterprise, Workday PI dashboard) · Views and natural light · Standing desks · Free coffee
Unique to this group
HIPAA-compliant HPC gap · PhD pipeline collapse · No journal club or academic seminar infrastructure · No formal accommodations process · Salary/contract tracking opaque · Postdoc-specific onboarding gap · Adjustable/dimmable lighting needed · Wired internet at desks · Desk assignment structure needed · First-floor classroom A/V unreliable · Room numbering and wayfinding confusing · Bike storage needed
Students
7 people
▸
Top pain points
No dedicated student space: Students want a space that is theirs — somewhere to be loud, socialize, and study without worrying about disturbing or being disturbed by professional events and meetings.
Wellness room access and misuse: Wellness rooms are frequently unavailable due to misuse. There is not enough semi-private study space, and event noise creates regular disruption.
Platform and resource onboarding: Students wish they had been introduced to key systems — Banner, Canvas, the library, and other campus resources — from day one rather than discovering them over time.
Top positives
Café and bookable spaces · Human front desk and student ambassador desk as social anchors · Discounted cafeteria food
Unique to this group
Fitness center and cafeteria as priorities · Wellness specialist on campus · Elevator signage confusion · Dedicated study area · More books and study materials
Roux Incubator Members
3 people
▸
Top pain points
Practical logistics are completely opaque: Basic campus information — closure schedules, system access, parking, how to reserve spaces — is not communicated to incubator members. Everything must be figured out independently through other people.
Isolation as solo founders and embedded users: Members come to campus primarily for connection, but the open concept does not always deliver the structured social infrastructure they need. The experience can feel lonely without intentional community-building.
Tool and software access gaps: Members rely heavily on tools outside the NU ecosystem. Sponsored account restrictions block access to platforms that would meaningfully support their work.
Top positives
Relationship-building and community · Views and physical environment · Energy from constantly changing events and people
Unique to this group
No Claude access on sponsored accounts · Specific physical amenity requests (scales, showers, nap pods) · No NU system onboarding for embedded/non-staff users
Co-ops
2 people
▸
Top pain points
Software and tool access: Key design platforms are not provided through NU, requiring significant self-learning with no mentorship or guidance on tooling.
No peer collaborator: Design work feels isolated. Having a colleague to ideate and problem-solve with is a clear unmet need.
No structured introduction to systems: Nothing is explained at the start of the placement. Co-ops must self-discover tools, processes, and resources over time.
Top positives
Open layout and visibility of collaboration across teams · Downtown location and walkability
Unique to this group
Design equipment gaps (graphic printers, compositing tools) · Experience changes with every cohort rotation
Findings raised across persona groups
5/5
Raised by every persona group
  • People and community are the primary draw to campus — present in every single interview across all groups
  • Systems don't talk to each other — manual data transfer is the norm across every team and role
4/5
Raised by 4 persona groups
  • Insufficient private and enclosed space — named in nearly every session and the most frequently cited infrastructure gap
  • Practical logistics are opaque — all groups default to asking a person rather than finding documentation
  • Onboarding is inconsistent and team-dependent — incubator members receive no onboarding at all
  • Edge cases and nuanced situations require a human — systems cannot handle context, urgency, or ambiguity
  • Claude is broadly valued as a work tool — named positively by staff, research, incubator, and co-op groups
3/5
Raised by 3 persona groups
  • The front desk must remain human — automating it would undermine the sense of welcome and belonging on campus
2/5
Raised by 2 persona groups
  • Informal knowledge brokers are overloaded — specific individuals are absorbing disproportionate institutional load that should be distributed or documented
  • AV/IT is a single point of failure — one person covering all technical support creates unacceptable operational risk
  • Room booking enforcement is needed — rooms appear available but are physically occupied with no mechanism to address this
Questions with thin data
Q6 — Accessibility
Even with the full corpus, most respondents gave vague answers or said they were unsure. The most concrete data point is a documented case of a prospective student with accessibility needs who did not come to Roux after receiving only a boilerplate response. Physical accessibility issues — including inaccessible door hardware and a need for dimmable lighting — were raised but not systematically explored. This question likely needs to be reframed in future rounds to surface more specific experiences.
Q4 — Human vs. system
Incubator and co-op respondents gave minimal responses to this question. It may have landed differently with non-staff groups who interact with campus systems less frequently.

🔴 Urgent & Immediate Action
1
Address Personnel Capacity Now
  • Backfill open roles in campus operations and events before taking on increased building capacity
  • Create a second AV/IT support position to eliminate the current single point of failure
  • Establish a formal flex-time policy for staff who cover evening and weekend events
  • Acknowledge and address burnout before it results in further departures
  • Reinstate regular all-staff touchpoints to rebuild cross-team communication and community
UrgentStaffHigh confidence
2
Build a centralized onboarding experience for all campus users
  • Create a standard onboarding process that applies to staff, researchers, incubator members, and co-ops
  • Include an org chart with team descriptions, goals, faces, and titles
  • Cover practical logistics: system access, parking, guest WiFi, campus closure schedule, who handles what
  • Implement a buddy program or structured welcome meetings modeled on the Boston campus approach
  • Develop a separate onboarding track for postdocs that addresses progression expectations and available resources
  • Create a version for incubator members who are not NU-affiliated but need access to campus systems
Immediate action4/5 personasHigh confidence
3
Integrate Systems to Eliminate Manual Work
  • Facilitate a working session to map all systems and identify the highest-value integration opportunities
  • Prioritize: Salesforce/Mailchimp, Tripleseat/Robin, NUWorks/Salesforce, newsletter/Mailchimp, SharePoint/Google Drive
  • Assign ownership and capacity to act on integration improvements — this cannot be done by teams already at capacity
Systems5/5 personasHigh confidence
4
Create a Central Knowledge Base
  • Build a single, maintained hub covering logistics, policies, and who handles what
  • Include a staff directory with roles, teams, and points of contact — especially important given recent turnover
  • Actively communicate where to find things; building it is not enough if people don't know it exists
  • Keep it current — fragmented and outdated documentation is worse than none
Immediate action4/5 personasHigh confidence
5
Enforce Room Booking and Space Use Policies
  • Require check-in on room touchpads and automatically release rooms that are not checked in
  • Publish and enforce clear policies on appropriate use of phone booths, meeting rooms, and wellness rooms
  • Designate a clear point of contact when spaces are used inappropriately
  • Address wellness room misuse specifically — napping is locking out people who need the space for pumping
Quick winResearch + staffHigh confidence

Deploy Envoy for Guest Management

  • Ensure guest notifications go to the right people, not just one point of contact
  • Use Envoy's existing features for parking validation and guest check-in before investing in other solutions
  • Complete full rollout before the new building opens

🔵 Future & New Building Considerations
1
New Building Needs a Spectrum of Spaces
  • Provide individual offices or bookable focus rooms for faculty and staff with heavy meeting loads
  • Include fully soundproof rooms for HR conversations, student crisis support, and sensitive discussions
  • Offer a range of phone booth sizes, including options for 2–3 people
  • Create a dedicated student zone that does not compete with professional event or work space
  • Include rooms with adjustable and dimmable lighting to support sensory and accessibility needs
  • Ensure all meeting room and bathroom doors have accessible hardware — no sliding doors
New building4/5 personasHigh confidence
2
Establish a Formal Accommodations Process
  • Designate a named point of contact for accommodation requests — make this publicly known
  • Document a process that mirrors the established Boston campus approach
  • Communicate proactively to prospective students, visitors, and new hires before they need to ask
  • Ensure accessible door hardware, dimmable lighting, and other physical features are built into the new building spec
New buildingResearch + staffMedium confidence
3
Build Academic Infrastructure for PhD Students
  • Establish a journal club, academic seminar series, and structured student community before the new building opens
  • Focus on building critical mass in one or two areas rather than trying to replicate the Boston model
  • Acknowledge that people coming to Maine have different community needs — design intentionally for that
  • Create a postdoc group with formal structure to ensure postdocs are getting what they need to progress
New buildingResearchMedium confidence
