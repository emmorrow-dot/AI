Staff
~34 people · 19 sessions incl. focus group
▸
Top pain points
Personnel capacity: Roles are not being backfilled after departures, leaving everyone doing more with less. There is no redundancy in IT or AV support, and burnout is a consistent theme across this group.
HR feels dehumanized: The shift to ticketing systems for HR removes the human connection people value. Staff want to reach a person they know, not submit a ticket into a queue.
Open office and lack of private space: Glass walls and an open layout make staff feel constantly accessible and interrupted, making focused work difficult. Work-from-home days are often the only opportunity for deep work.
System fragmentation: Teams rely on manual data transfer between disconnected platforms. There is no bandwidth or organizational support to address the integrations needed.
Unclear processes and definitions: Staff struggle to find information about event definitions, fee structures, benefits, and who handles what — especially for Boston-based policies. People default to asking colleagues rather than finding documentation.
Top positives
Community and colleagues · Physical environment · Hybrid flexibility · Internal communications that surface accomplishments and events help reduce isolation
Unique to this group
Envoy underdeployment · Donor data inaccessibility · Burnout as a retention risk · Loss of weekly team meetings · Event Intake Form reliability · Student employee turnover creating inconsistent event support · Social connection gaps (Fikas valued; more informal gatherings needed) · CRM/Salesforce limitations for tracking students and programs · Admissions presence gap at entrance — no dedicated person for walk-in visitors · Event definitions unclear (internal vs. external, fees vs. in-kind) · Secure storage gap for sensitive documents · Accessibility gaps: braille signage, wheelchair-friendly setups, phone booth access

Top pain points
No dedicated student space: Students want a space that is theirs — somewhere to be loud, socialize, and study without worrying about disturbing or being disturbed by professional events and meetings.
Wellness room access and misuse: Wellness rooms are frequently unavailable due to misuse. There is not enough semi-private study space, and event noise creates regular disruption.

People & Staffing
Critical
Roles not backfilled — staffing at breaking point
Unanimous across all staff interviews. Research shows universities that operate with chronic understaffing see 2–3x higher turnover rates and significant declines in service quality. EDUCAUSE benchmarks identify staffing redundancy as a top operational resilience requirement.
Critical
AV/IT single point of failure — no redundancy
One person covering all technical support is flagged as an operational risk across both staff and research groups. EDUCAUSE identifies single points of failure in IT as among the highest-risk campus infrastructure gaps, particularly as institutions scale event programming.
Important
No standardized onboarding — team-dependent experience
Raised across staff, research, students, and co-ops. SHRM research shows employees who experience structured onboarding are 69% more likely to stay for 3+ years. McKinsey identifies decentralized onboarding in universities as a top compliance and retention risk. UNC Chapel Hill and University of Minnesota both cite standardized onboarding as a strategic priority.
Important
Postdoc-specific onboarding gap
Postdocs receive significantly less information than faculty about progression and resources. Best practice at peer institutions (MIT, Johns Hopkins, UC system) includes a dedicated postdoc onboarding track and formal postdoc association with structured check-ins.
Important
Informal knowledge brokers overloaded
Specific individuals absorbing disproportionate institutional load. This is a documented retention risk — when these individuals leave, institutional knowledge disappears. Best practice is knowledge mapping and documented SOPs before key staff transitions.
Space & Facilities
Critical
Insufficient private and enclosed space
Named in nearly every session — the most frequently mentioned infrastructure gap in the entire corpus. SCUP (Society for College and University Planning) benchmarks show that high-performing research campuses allocate 25–35% of workspace to private or semi-private settings. Purely open-plan offices are increasingly cited in higher ed facilities literature as counterproductive for research and sensitive student work.
Critical
No private space for sensitive conversations
Glass-walled rooms are inadequate for HR conversations, student crisis support, and financial aid discussions. FERPA and Title IX compliance standards require private spaces for sensitive student interactions. Peer institutions (e.g. Northeastern Boston) provide dedicated private rooms for these purposes as a baseline.
Important
Room booking enforcement gap
Rooms show as available but are physically occupied with no enforcement mechanism. Best practice at peer institutions uses touchpad check-in with automatic release after 10–15 minutes of no check-in. MIT, Stanford, and Cornell all employ automated room release systems.
Important
Wellness room misuse — pumping rooms inaccessible
Wellness rooms being used for napping, locking out people who need them for pumping. The PUMP Act (2022) requires reasonable break time and private space for nursing employees. This is a legal compliance issue, not just a convenience gap.
Important
No dedicated student space
Students want a space separate from professional events and work. ACPA and NASPA student affairs benchmarks identify dedicated student commons as a core belonging and retention infrastructure. Institutions without them consistently report lower student satisfaction and engagement scores.
Nice to Have
Outdoor space, showers, bike storage, gym access
Raised by multiple groups. WELL Building Standard benchmarks flag shower access as important for active commuters. Increasingly common in peer innovation campuses (e.g. MIT Media Lab, CMU IDEATE). Quality of life amenities that support retention and recruitment.
Systems & Technology
Critical
Systems don't integrate — manual data transfer is the norm
Named across all 5 persona groups. EDUCAUSE 2024 benchmarks identify system integration as the #1 unmet technology need in higher education operations. Institutions with 25+ disconnected platforms (as reported here) are in the highest-risk tier for data loss, duplication errors, and staff burnout.
Critical
HIPAA-compliant HPC unavailable
The current HPC cluster is not HIPAA-compliant, blocking health and life sciences research. This is a legal compliance issue. Peer research institutions (BU, Tufts, Brown) all provide HIPAA-compliant computing environments as a baseline for health research faculty.
Important
Software fatigue — 25+ platforms, multiple passwords
Raised in focus groups. EDUCAUSE research shows staff cognitive load from platform switching reduces productivity by up to 20%. Best practice is platform consolidation and single sign-on (SSO) implementation — a standard feature at most peer R1 institutions.
Important
CRM/Salesforce not fit for student tracking
Raised by multiple staff across Jim's interviews. Salesforce is widely used in higher ed but requires significant customization for student pathways. Peer institutions (Northeastern Boston, Emerson, Suffolk) use Salesforce Education Cloud or Slate as purpose-built alternatives.
Important
SOPs person-dependent — no centralized documentation
Raised in focus groups. When key staff leave, institutional knowledge leaves with them. Best practice is a living SOP repository (Confluence, SharePoint, or Notion) with assigned ownership and regular review cycles. Northeastern Boston is already moving in this direction.
Nice to Have
Envoy underdeployed for guest management
The tool exists and is already paid for. Full deployment is a quick win requiring only an SOP and training, not new investment.
Information & Knowledge Access
Critical
No central knowledge base — logistics opaque across all groups
Every group defaults to asking a person. This creates overload on informal knowledge brokers and inconsistent answers. EDUCAUSE and NACUBO identify a centralized, searchable knowledge base as a baseline operational requirement for satellite campuses. Absence correlates with lower staff confidence and higher error rates.
Important
Org chart and team descriptions missing or unknown
Multiple staff can't name colleagues on adjacent teams. Best practice at peer institutions includes a published org chart with team descriptions, contact roles, and a staff directory — updated quarterly. This is standard at all Northeastern global campuses except Portland.
Important
Event definitions and fee structures unclear
Staff can't determine when to charge fees, use contracts, or treat events as co-hosted. This creates legal and financial risk. Peer institutions publish clear event policy matrices distinguishing internal, external, co-hosted, and VIP event categories with corresponding fee and contract requirements.
Important
Emergency response protocols missing
Front desk staff have no protocol for high-stakes situations including federal officials arriving on campus and students in crisis. Clery Act compliance requires documented emergency response procedures. This is a legal and safety gap — every accredited institution must have documented cross-departmental crisis response protocols.
Accessibility & Inclusion
Critical
No formal accommodations process
A prospective PhD student did not come to Roux because no accommodation guidance existed. ADA Title II (updated 2024) and Section 504 require institutions receiving federal funding to have a named contact and documented process. Deadline for compliance: April 2026–2027 depending on size. This is a legal requirement, not optional.
Critical
Phone booths and physical spaces inaccessible to wheelchair users
Phone booth lips/steps, narrow widths, and high-top reception tables exclude wheelchair users. ADA requires accessible routes and facilities. These must be addressed in the new building design brief — retrofitting after construction is significantly more expensive.
Important
No braille signage or accessible wayfinding
ADA Standards for Accessible Design require braille on room identification signage. Color-dependent systems (keypads) present barriers for sight-impaired users. This is a compliance gap that should be addressed in the current building and built into new building specs.
Important
Parking equity — cost is a barrier for lower-salaried staff
Tiered parking pricing by salary is a widely adopted model at peer institutions (MIT, Harvard, UMass) to ensure lower-paid staff are not disproportionately burdened. Remote work flexibility is also flagged as an equity issue for those who cannot easily afford to commute.
Nice to Have
Adjustable/dimmable lighting and sensory accommodations
Raised by research faculty and staff. WELL Building Standard v2 recommends tunable lighting in workspaces. Increasingly standard in new higher education buildings as a low-cost, high-impact accommodation.
Community & Culture
Important
PhD student community infrastructure missing
No journal club, academic seminar series, or structured PhD community. CGS (Council of Graduate Schools) standards identify these as baseline requirements for doctoral programs. Absence contributes to attrition — doctoral completion rates drop significantly without peer community infrastructure.
Important
No feedback loop built into events and operations
Live feedback collection achieves 80–90% response rates vs. ~20% for post-event surveys. Peer institutions with structured feedback cultures (MIT, Stanford) report significantly higher operational improvement rates. Building feedback loops into all events is a low-cost, high-return investment.
Nice to Have
Social infrastructure — Fikas, informal gatherings, community norms
Community-building programming is consistently linked to retention in higher education research. Informal touchpoints (Fikas, potlucks, mixer events) were cited positively when they existed and missed when discontinued. Low cost, high cultural impact.

Ideal State for Infrastructure & Operations — 5-Year Vision
Synthesized from multiple focus groups facilitated by Liv Berg and Annie Chuprevich · ~20 participants · Whiteboard session
Flagged Gaps Requiring Immediate Attention
  • Emergency response protocols are a critical gap — cross-departmental training is needed for situations like a student in crisis
  • Federal officials arrived on campus and the front-desk student had no clear protocol for how to respond
  • Single people are doing multiple roles with no backup — when they leave, everything they carry leaves with them
SOPs & Knowledge Management
  • SOPs should be centralized, up to date, and connected to Boston — with a clear rationale for where Roux aligns or diverges
  • Currently at risk of being person-dependent — when key people leave, institutional knowledge walks out with them
  • Confluence flagged as a potential home for SOPs; the Boston team is already moving in this direction
  • SOPs should be loaded into AI tools so staff can query them in real time — especially useful for front desk students handling unexpected situations
  • A search/chat/CRM model for the knowledge base (e.g. Ask Fin chatbot) is the right direction, even if imperfect today
Systems & Software
  • Aim for a minimum viable number of software platforms with maximum integration between them
  • Students and staff are experiencing significant software fatigue — 25+ systems, multiple passwords, constant context-switching
  • New event software is incoming and is seen as adding to the load rather than reducing it
  • Event management should not be automated — it requires human judgment to validate that requests make sense
Staffing & Redundancy
  • No single point of failure per function — skills and knowledge should be mapped and shared, not assumed
  • The new campus will be significantly larger (two major buildings, waterfront/pier) — current staffing levels are already insufficient
  • Co-ops and students are filling operational gaps but cycle out — this is not a sustainable model
  • Informal expertise ("they know that") should not substitute for proper role definition and compensation
  • Lab facilities need dedicated staff for preventative maintenance to remain operationally sustainable
Facilities & Community Norms
  • More customized facilities — adjustable desks, automated HVAC based on occupancy
  • Clearer community norms for campus use and shared spaces — students are using designated spaces incorrectly (e.g. nursing mothers' room)
  • Parking equity needs to be addressed — tiered pricing by salary proposed (below threshold = free; above = pay)
  • Current parking system is unreliable — gate arm failures with no one answering the support line
Feedback Culture & AI Opportunities
  • Build a feedback loop into all events and processes: collect → synthesize → share back → act — all three steps need to be resourced
  • Live in-session feedback collection achieves ~80–90% response rates vs. ~20% when left to self-completion
  • AI can help synthesize feedback: upload responses, surface top trends with supporting quotes
  • Quantitative feedback needed from all campus users — not just staff, but students, co-ops, and community visitors
